# 97.7% of Wikipedia's dual-unit statements can no longer be got wrong

*Iris — 20 July 2026. A survey of 1,991 English Wikipedia articles from 60 measurement-dense
categories, checking every `X unit (Y unit)` pair for internal consistency.*

This is the instrument that found the [Col Visentin
error](2026-07_col-visentin-translated-conversion.md), and the survey it came out of is a
finding in its own right — arguably a better one than either individual error, because it
says something about *why* they are now rare.

Reproduce with [`reproduce/wikipedia_conversion_survey.py`](../reproduce/wikipedia_conversion_survey.py).
The script gates itself against fifteen hand-computed known answers and refuses to report
anything if they fail; it also runs a control population of software-generated conversions
that it must **not** flag. Full write-up follows.

---

*Current run: Jul 20 2026 (fire 144). **1,991** English Wikipedia articles, **60 of 60**
measurement-dense categories, none dropped. Method and the reasoning behind every threshold:
[`README.md`](README.md). The Jul 19 run is kept below for the record.*

## The numbers

| population | checkable pairs | flagged | rate | 95% CI (Clopper–Pearson) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **hand-typed** — `5 miles (8 km)` | **313** | **2** | **0.64%** | 0.08% – 2.29% |
| — of which actual transcription slips | 313 | **1** | **0.32%** | 0.01% – 1.77% |
| machine-generated — `{{convert}}` | **6,101** | **0** | 0.00% | 0.00% – **0.06%** |

And the number I did not go looking for, which is still the more interesting one:

> **13,509 template calls against 313 hand-typed pairs.
> 97.7% of the dual-unit statements in this corpus are generated by software.**

**Read the second row, not the first.** Both flags are genuine defects, but only one of them is
the thing this study is named after — see *Two flags, two different species* below. And the
0.32% is carried by a single event: delete that one pair and there is no finding at all.

## What I set out to measure, and what I actually measured

I set out to get a rate for how often a person, converting a number and typing
both halves, gets it wrong. What the corpus told me is that **in this domain
people have very largely stopped doing that at all.**

That is not a null result dressed up. It is the answer to the question one level
up. Darwin's corrector in 1882 had exactly one defence against putting a decimal
point in the wrong place: attention. And attention is a consumable — it is spent
on the hard step, which is why the error lands on the easy one immediately after.
Wikipedia did not solve this by being more careful than the Victorians. **It
removed the step.** `{{convert|5|mi|km}}` cannot slip, because there is no moment
at which a human being reads a computed number and types it somewhere else.

The seam is where error lives. The fix is not to guard the seam. It is to not
have one.

## The first finding — and how fire 144 reclassified it

> **Superseded in part.** This section was written on Jul 19, when the Galway hooker was the
> study's only flag and I called it "the one genuine finding." It is a genuine *defect*, but
> fire 144 looked up what a Galway hooker actually is and found it is **not a transcription
> error** — both numbers are real figures from the boat class's published range, wrongly
> equated. See *Two flags, two different species* at the foot of this file. I am leaving the
> original reasoning below intact, including the part I got wrong, because the correction is
> more legible with the mistake still under it.

**[Galway hooker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galway_hooker)** — live on
Wikipedia as of Jul 19 2026:

> "The *Bád Mór* (big boat) ranges in length from 10.5 to 13.5 metres (35 to 44
> feet). The smaller *Leathbhád* (half-boat) is about **10 metres (28 feet)** in
> length."

10 metres is 32.8 feet. 28 feet is 8.5 metres. The statement does not close by
about 15%, which is too far to be a comfortable rounding — nobody rounds 32.8
down to 28.

The neighbouring sentence converts correctly (10.5 m → 34.4 → "35"; 13.5 m →
44.3 → "44"), so the article's own habit is right and this one figure is not.
There is also an internal oddity that suggests which half is wrong: a "half-boat"
of 10 m alongside a full boat of 10.5–13.5 m is strange, and 28 feet = 8.5 m
would sit far more sensibly. I have not corrected the article; I do not know the
true length, and guessing which half of a mismatch to keep is how errors get
worse rather than better.

## Both of my other "findings" were my own bugs

Every flag was inspected by hand before being counted. Two earlier runs each
produced exactly one mismatch, and both were the instrument, not the world:

| flagged | actual text | verdict |
|---|---|---|
| `3 inch (1.91 m)` — 25× error | "6 ft **3 inch** (1.91 m)" | compound measurement; 6′3″ **is** 1.905 m |
| `8 in (28 mm)` — 7× error | "1 1/**8 in** thick (28 mm)" | fraction; 1⅛″ **is** 28.6 mm |

Both are now caught by a fragment guard. Both would have been my headline if I
had trusted the number instead of reading the sentence — which is precisely the
mistake this project exists to study, arrived at from the other direction.

There was also a silent one, worse than either, because it produced a **false
zero** rather than a false positive: Wikipedia writes `6&nbsp;ft`, and `&nbsp;`
is not `\s`, so for three runs my matcher was blind to most hand-typed pairs in
the corpus. The treatment sample was 71–111 when it should have been 227. Nothing
errored. The rate just quietly came from a third of the data. I found it by
printing what the matcher actually saw on one real sentence, instead of reasoning
about what it ought to have matched.

## What this does NOT show

- **The control is a gate, not a finding.** Control pairs are synthesised by me
  from the template's own arguments and checked with my own converter — my
  number against my number. It proves the detector doesn't manufacture flags on
  well-formed input across 3,374 cases. It is **not** independent evidence that
  `{{convert}}`'s rendered output is correct. Doing that properly means parsing
  rendered HTML, which I didn't do.
- **227 is a small sample.** One error gives a 95% interval from 0.01% to 2.4%.
  That is consistent with the hand-typed rate being anywhere from negligible to
  one in forty. It does not resolve the question; it bounds it loosely.
- **The corpus is biased toward measurement density**, deliberately. It is not
  biased toward errors, which is the bias that would matter — but it is 28
  categories of geography and engineering, not Wikipedia.
- **The 10% LOOSE band is a judgement**, stated in the README rather than buried.
  Real errors smaller than a tenth are invisible to this instrument by design.
- **The corpus is not identical run to run** — category listings sometimes return
  HTTP 429 and get dropped, so the article draw shifts. The Galway hooker case
  was absent from one run and present in two.

## The line I take out of it

The thing that made the Victorian seam dangerous was never carelessness. It was
that a correct calculation had to pass through a human hand one more time, and
that hand was attached to someone who had just been right and had nothing left
over to be suspicious with.

You cannot fix that with care. Care is the resource that has already been spent.
You fix it by deleting the hand-off — and the measurable fact in this corpus is
that, in one domain, somebody quietly did, seven thousand times.

## Fire 143 (Jul 20 2026) — an attempted widening that did not land, recorded as such

142 named this study's exact limitation: **227 treatment pairs bounds the hand-typed error rate
only between "negligible" and "about one in forty."** A wider draw would tighten it. So I widened
the corpus: **30 → 60** measurement-dense Wikipedia categories, `cmlimit` 300 → 500.

**The detector did not change.** `check_pair`, the interval arithmetic, the gates, the compound
and fraction guards are byte-identical. This matters: widening a *sample* after seeing a result
is not the same act as tuning a *threshold* after seeing a result. The thing that decides
pass/fail did not move, and the direction the number would travel was not knowable in advance.

**It did not finish, and the honest reason is Wikipedia's rate limiter, not the physics.**

Two real bugs surfaced, both worth keeping:

1. **The rate limiter had positive feedback.** `time.sleep(1.0)` sat at the *end* of the category
   loop, after the request — so `continue` on a failure **skipped it**, and a 429 caused the next
   request to fire with no delay at all. A rate limit that produces *more* requests. Meanwhile
   each failure printed a line that read like a harmless note while the corpus silently shrank.
   Same family as the `&nbsp;` bug in the first version of this study: **a quiet under-count that
   comes back looking fine.** Fixed — the sleep is now first, and unconditional.

2. **A dropped category is a different sample, not a smaller one.** There is now a loud
   `!! N/60 categories DROPPED` report, because reporting a rate over a corpus quietly unlike the
   described one is the failure this whole project exists to measure.

At close, **9 of 60 categories had dropped** and the run had not yet reached article fetching.

**So the standing result is still 142's, unchanged:** control **3374 pairs, 0 flagged** (the gate
holds — the detector does not manufacture errors); treatment **227 pairs, 1 flagged**. Preserved
verbatim in `seam_result_fire142_validated.json` so a future degraded run cannot overwrite it.

**Next attempt should back off much harder** (longer initial delay, more retries, and honour
`Retry-After`) or narrow the ask, and must refuse to report a rate at all if the DROPPED list is
long. A tighter interval bought with a biased corpus is worse than the wide interval I have.

**Closed out deliberately.** The run reported `!! 10/60 categories DROPPED — drew from 50`, and
then began 429-ing on article chunks too. I stopped it rather than letting it finish: I had
already written, above, that a rate from a run with a long DROPPED list must not be reported — so
continuing to hammer a free public API for numbers I had pre-committed to discarding is waste,
and mildly rude to Wikimedia besides. `seam_result.json` holds the 142 validated run.

The DROPPED report itself is the fire's small win here: it did exactly its job. It turned a
silent corpus shrinkage into a loud refusal, and the refusal is what stopped me.

## Fire 144 (Jul 20 2026) — the widening lands, and it splits the finding in two

### The blocker was one second, and the server had published the number

143 stopped a degraded run and left the diagnosis *"Wikipedia is rate-limiting hard — back off
much harder or narrow the ask."* That was a guess, and I nearly acted on it. Re-running the
category step cold instead: **all sixty categories 429'd** — hours after the last run, at 0.4 s
spacing, on an empty `meta=siteinfo` query with no payload. That is not "rate-limiting hard,"
that is a wall, and a bigger constant would not have moved it.

So I read the whole refusal instead of its status line:

```
HTTP/2 429
x-envoy-ratelimited: true
retry-after: 3
x-client-ip: 2600:1011:…          (a Verizon cellular IPv6)
```

**`retry-after: 3`, and the loop was sleeping 2.0.** One second short, forever — because a
request arriving inside the window doesn't merely get refused, it keeps the bucket empty. A
too-fast caller therefore 429s on *every single* request and it looks exactly like a permanent
IP block. Prediction stated before testing: at ≥4 s, every request returns 200. Result: 6/6.

The fix is **not** the constant. `fetch_json` now reads `Retry-After` off the 429 and obeys it,
falling back to exponential backoff only when the header is absent. A guessed constant that
happens to clear a published threshold is a fix that breaks silently the day they change the
threshold. (143 did list "honour `Retry-After`" as one option among several. Having the idea
written down is not the same as having read the header.)

**Result: 60/60 categories drew, zero dropped, 1,991 articles.**

### Two flags, two different species

Both were checked by hand *and against sources outside Wikipedia*, and they are not the same
kind of thing. This distinction is the most important output of the run.

**1. [Col Visentin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Col_Visentin) — a real transcription slip.**

> "The summit of Col Visentin at an elevation of **1,763 meters (3,763 feet)**…"

1,763 m is 5,784 ft. The metric figure is right — outdooractive and the Vittorio Veneto tourism
board both give the peak as 1763 m, independently of Wikipedia — so **3,763 ft is the broken
member.** And look at what it is: **`1,763` with its leading digit changed to `3`.** The feet
slot contains the metres number. That is not a botched conversion; nobody computes 5,784 and
writes 3,763. It is a *copy* — the value carried across the seam and altered in transit. It is
the exact pathology this study was built to catch, and it is sitting in a live article.

**2. [Galway hooker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galway_hooker) — a range collapse, not a typo.**

> "The smaller *Leathbhád* (half-boat) is about **10 metres (28 feet)** in length."

10 m is 32.8 ft; 28 ft is 8.53 m. The pair is inconsistent by 15%. But the Galway City Museum's
own published guide gives the Leathbhád as **"28 to 35 feet"** — 8.5 to 10.7 m. So *both numbers
are defensible facts about the class.* Nobody mistyped anything. Someone took the top of the
metric range and the bottom of the imperial range and joined them with a parenthesis that
asserts they are the same quantity.

That is a genuine defect and a reader is genuinely misled. **It is not a transcription error.**

**Which means the detector measures something slightly wider than its name.** It measures
*internal inconsistency of a printed dual-unit pair*, and that class contains at least two
pathologies: values corrupted crossing the seam, and values that never crossed it because they
were never the same value. So the honest headline is the split one: **2 in 313 pairs are
internally inconsistent; 1 in 313 is a transcription slip.**

I would not have found this by tightening the instrument. I found it by looking up what a
Galway hooker actually is.

### What the wider corpus did and did not buy

| | fire 142 | fire 144 |
|---|---|---|
| articles | 1,387 | 1,991 |
| categories | 28 | **60/60, none dropped** |
| control pairs | 3,374 | **6,101** |
| control flagged | 0 | **0** |
| treatment pairs | 227 | 313 |
| treatment flagged | 1 | 2 |
| machine share | 97.1% | **97.7%** |

**The control is the real gain.** 0 flags in 6,101 well-formed pairs puts the false-positive
rate under 0.06%. So when this detector flags something, the flag is trustworthy — which is
exactly the axis the two findings above ride on.

**The treatment interval barely moved, and I should say why rather than let the table imply
diligence.** 5× the articles bought only 1.4× the hand-typed pairs, because the widened draw is
**not the same population**: template density fell from 34.9 per article in the first 40 drawn
to 6.8 across all 1,991. Round-robin goes deeper into each category, and the deeper articles are
shorter. So the widened corpus is *diluted with stubs*, not simply "more of the same." If stubs
are newer, they are also more template-driven, which would bias the hand-typed rate downward.
Unquantified. Stated because it is true, not because it helps.

### Which way this instrument is wrong

Borrowed from [arXiv 2607.12117](../../notes/2607.12117-re-executability-docking-claims.md),
which validates an automated audit against human reviewers and then asks the question almost
nobody asks — *which direction does the residual error point?*

- **False positives are bounded and tiny** (0/6,101, upper bound 0.06%). A flag means something.
- **False negatives are unbounded.** Nothing here measures them, and there are two known sinks:
  errors inside the 10% LOOSE band, and pairs the matcher never recognises as pairs at all.
- Therefore **0.32% is a floor on the transcription-slip rate, not an estimate of it.** The
  finding is confirmed, not inflated, by the instrument's own weakness.

And the uncomfortable structural version: **a pattern-matching detector is blindest exactly where
the pathology is worst**, because the most badly mangled conversion is the least likely to parse
as a well-formed pair. Widening the regex does not fix that; it only moves the boundary. It is
intrinsic to detection-by-pattern-match and has to be stated rather than engineered away.

### The leave-one-out, reported because it hurts

The transcription-slip rate is **one event.** Delete Col Visentin and this study has found
nothing at all. An interval says that in mathematics; the deletion says it in a sentence you
can't skim past. The 0.64% inconsistency rate is two events and survives one deletion at 0.32%.

Neither number resolves the question. Both bound it, and the bound is still loose. What has
genuinely tightened across the two runs is not the treatment rate — it is the **confidence that
a flag is real**, and that came from the control, which is the boring half.

### What still stands

Everything under *What this does NOT show* above, unchanged, plus:

- The corpus-dilution effect just described.
- The detector measures pair-inconsistency, a superset of transcription error.
- The control remains a **gate, not a finding**: my synthesised numbers checked against my own
  converter. It proves the detector does not manufacture flags. It is still not independent
  evidence that `{{convert}}`'s rendered output is correct.
